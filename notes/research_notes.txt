Research notes
==============

Objective
---------
Build a futures signal/backtest notebook that can run unchanged when the same
train_set files are replaced with data extended through February 2026.

Design choices
--------------
1. Use adjusted C1 prices for PnL.
   The instruction file says adjusted prices can be differenced directly and
   multiplied by 5,000 bushels for one-lot PnL. That makes train_adjPrices1.csv
   the clean trading return series.

2. Use unadjusted C1/C2 prices for curve features.
   The C1-C2 spread and ratio are economically meaningful measures of nearby
   tightness, carry, contango, or backwardation. They are not used directly for
   PnL because the instruction file says the adjusted series is the correct
   continuous PnL series.

3. Respect release lags.
   Public inventories and receipts are lagged by two calendar days. Cargill
   inventory and Cargill crush activity are lagged by one calendar day. COT is
   lagged conservatively by three calendar days. All lagged features are then
   forward-filled onto the adjusted-price trading calendar.

4. Start with an interpretable model.
   The notebook uses a Ridge regression implemented with numpy. This avoids
   depending on scikit-learn and remains compatible with Python 3.6. Ridge was
   chosen because feature blocks are correlated: momentum, curve, inventory,
   receipts, and COT variables can overlap in what they explain.

5. Use a cross-sectional portfolio.
   Daily predictions are risk-adjusted, demeaned across the four commodities,
   and scaled to one aggregate gross lot. This reduces broad grain beta and
   focuses the backtest on relative signals.

Signals attempted in this first implementation
----------------------------------------------
- 20-day and 60-day price momentum.
- 5-day short-term reversal.
- 20-day realized volatility.
- C1-C2 curve spread and ratio.
- 20-day change in the curve spread.
- COT money-manager level and change.
- COT producer/merchant open-interest-normalized level and change.
- Public inventory level and change.
- Public receipts level and change.
- Cargill inventory level and change.
- Cargill crush processed, planned, processed-minus-planned, and utilization.

What worked so far
------------------
The improved version uses a five-day Ridge target with a small price/curve/COT
core and a heavily regularized physical overlay. Adding an expanding median edge
filter improved 2018-2020 out-of-sample Sharpe from -0.460 for the first broad
Ridge model to 1.498, and it also beats the simple 60-day momentum baseline's
0.619 out-of-sample Sharpe.

To check overfit, I added expanding walk-forward versions that fit only on data
available before each rebalance window. The first quarterly 5-day version was too
weak, so I tested slower horizons and retraining frequencies. The better
anti-overfit check is now an annual walk-forward 20-day Ridge. It has 2018-2020
out-of-sample Sharpe of 1.347 and full-period Sharpe of 0.738.

What did not work yet
---------------------
The first broad one-day Ridge model overfit badly. It had a very high in-sample
Sharpe but negative out-of-sample Sharpe. Putting every feature, including
Cargill crush, into the same unconstrained model was too fragile.

The improved annual walk-forward model is still much stronger after 2018 than
before 2018. That is a real regime risk, not something to hide with an
event-specific rule.

Next tests
----------
1. Add walk-forward fitting instead of one static train/test split.
2. Test soybean-specific crush models.
3. Add seasonal normalizations for inventories and receipts by week-of-year.
4. Add transaction-cost sensitivity once realistic per-lot assumptions are known.
5. Test whether the same five-day horizon remains best on the February 2026
   extension.
6. Add a 2022 Russia/Ukraine war period test once the extended dataset is
   available.
7. Treat 2021-February 2026 as the true holdout. Do not tune the edge threshold
   or feature set again after seeing that result without creating a new holdout.

Additional model tests
----------------------
I tested statsmodels OLS and a Kalman-style recursive least-squares model. The
first Kalman run looked very strong, but I found and rejected a lookahead issue:
it updated on forward returns before those returns would have been known. After
fixing the timing, Kalman/RLS did not beat annual Ridge. OLS had a decent
full-period Sharpe but much weaker out-of-sample Sharpe. Ridge remains the final
walk-forward validation model.

Additional feature/portfolio tests
----------------------------------
I also tested seasonal normalization for physical data, soybean-specific crush
features, and directional/hybrid portfolio construction. Seasonal physical
features improved full-period Sharpe in a simplified annual model but reduced
out-of-sample Sharpe and PnL compared with the annual Ridge walk-forward
benchmark. Soybean-only crush and directional/hybrid portfolio construction did
not improve results. These are logged as rejected or future-extension ideas, not
final strategy changes.

Ideas adapted from oil/gas/FX research
--------------------------------------
I tested EWMA smoothing, weak-signal thresholds, top-N portfolios, volatility
targeting, crisis caps, and a stress-dependent trend/mean-reversion rule sleeve.
The rule sleeve produced high OOS Sharpe but much larger drawdown and turnover.
Risk-controlled versions were cleaner but did not beat the annual Ridge
walk-forward on the main balance of OOS PnL, full-period robustness, and model
simplicity. These are logged as diagnostics, not final changes.

Holding period and family weighting
-----------------------------------
The model predicts multi-day returns but currently recalculates positions daily.
I tested explicit 2/3/5/10/20-day holding periods through less frequent
rebalancing and staggered holdings. A 2-day hold helped the static candidate, but
it did not improve the annual walk-forward validation on OOS Sharpe/PnL. I also
tested equal weighting across price, positioning, and fundamental families. The
family-balanced approach was conceptually cleaner but empirically weaker than
the current Ridge walk-forward.

ML algorithm tests
------------------
I tested sklearn ML alternatives under the same annual walk-forward discipline:
Ridge/ElasticNet/Lasso/Huber, shallow Random Forests, Extra Trees, Gradient
Boosting, HistGradientBoosting, KNN, SVR, and a validation-MSE selector. I tested
both complete-row data and median imputation with missing-value indicators.

The best imputed ML model was shallow Gradient Boosting with a 10-day target:
OOS Sharpe 1.274 and OOS PnL 10,450. That was close, but still below the annual
Ridge walk-forward benchmark of OOS Sharpe 1.347 and OOS PnL 11,579. It also had
higher turnover and lower full-period Sharpe. Because the sample is small,
physical features are sparse, and nonlinear trees can split on regime-specific
noise, I did not promote ML into the final strategy.

Online research follow-up
-------------------------
I checked public commodity-futures research and tested the most relevant ideas:
momentum plus term structure, hedging pressure/COT, storage/inventory, basis
momentum, multi-factor combinations, lower-frequency rebalancing, staggered
holdings, and edge filters.

The literature-style factors contained signal, but standalone versions were
fragile in this four-commodity grain dataset. The strongest standalone factor,
price + term structure + hedging pressure + storage, had OOS Sharpe 1.468 but
only full Sharpe 0.265 and a max drawdown of -11,814, so it was not robust.

The best near-improvement was an expanding 60th-percentile edge filter on the
annual Ridge walk-forward predictions. It improved OOS Sharpe from 1.347 to
1.611 and reduced turnover from 0.234 to 0.200, but lowered OOS PnL from 11,579
to 9,863. A pre-2018 validation check did not uniquely select the 60th-percentile
threshold, so promoting it would risk mild OOS tuning. I logged it as a future
holdout-test idea, not a final strategy change.

Spread instrument extension
---------------------------
I added a separate spread-instrument experiment. Calendar spreads are now tested
as long front/short second adjusted futures PnL, while inter-commodity spreads
are tested as volatility-hedged long/short pairs across grains. Crack/crush
spreads were not tested as tradable instruments because the dataset does not
include soybean meal/oil futures prices.

Calendar spreads were too weak: OOS Sharpe 0.176 and OOS PnL 188. Inter-commodity
spreads had real but weaker standalone signal: OOS Sharpe 0.768 and OOS PnL
7,496. The best practical result was using inter-commodity spreads as a modest
overlay. An 80% outright / 20% inter-commodity overlay improved OOS Sharpe from
1.347 to 1.416, full Sharpe from 0.738 to 0.836, and max drawdown from -4,001 to
-3,615, but OOS PnL fell from 11,579 to 10,763. I would treat this as an
extension candidate for the 2021-February 2026 holdout, not as a replacement for
the current final strategy yet.

Cargill usage and holding-period cleanup
----------------------------------------
The main outright strategy uses both Cargill datasets. `cgl_inv` becomes
inventory level/change features, and `cgl_crush` becomes processed/planned,
surprise, and utilization features. The static and walk-forward Ridge models use
both through the physical overlay. Some later diagnostics, especially
literature-style factors and spread-instrument tests, used Cargill inventory but
not crush because the crush file is aggregate processed/planned data rather than
a clean commodity-pair or calendar-spread tradable input.

I moved the holding-period mechanics into the module. The result is unchanged:
2-day skip-rebalance improves the static edge-filtered candidate, lifting OOS
Sharpe from 1.498 to 1.730 and reducing turnover from 0.234 to 0.161. The
walk-forward anti-overfit model does not improve with holding periods, so the
2-day version remains a promising execution variant rather than the official
final model.

Filter and sleeve tests
-----------------------
I tested prediction-dispersion filters, 2/5-day holding periods, position
smoothing, a multi-condition prediction/curve/momentum dispersion filter, and a
simple carry/storage sleeve. The negative crisis period for the current static
strategy is the US-China trade war, where baseline Sharpe is -0.045.

The strongest result was the multi-condition filter. The validation-selected
version uses 40th-percentile expanding thresholds for prediction dispersion,
curve dispersion, and momentum dispersion. It improved OOS Sharpe from 1.498 to
2.098 and US-China Sharpe from -0.045 to 1.496, but OOS PnL fell from 10,304 to
7,556 because it trades fewer high-opportunity days. A 50th-percentile version
had even higher OOS Sharpe, but is more OOS-tuned.

The 2-day skip-rebalance also improved both the whole strategy and the negative
period: OOS Sharpe 1.730, OOS PnL 11,637, turnover 0.161, and US-China Sharpe
0.517. Position smoothing and the simple carry/storage sleeve helped selected
risk or crisis metrics but did not improve the whole strategy enough to promote.

Cost and margin stress test
---------------------------
I added cost/margin stress cases with explicit CDF, trade cost, and holding cost
columns. CDF is total cost divided by absolute gross PnL over active days. The
market-assumption case uses 8.75 dollars per one-way lot traded and 5% annual
margin funding. Margin assumptions are approximate per-lot stress inputs, not
broker quotes.

After market costs, 2-day skip-rebalance remains the best OOS PnL candidate
with OOS Sharpe 1.550 and OOS PnL 10,439. The multi-condition filter remains the
best Sharpe/crisis candidate with OOS Sharpe 1.801 and full Sharpe 1.488. The
annual walk-forward Ridge is more cost-sensitive, falling to OOS Sharpe 1.058
under the market-cost case.

I also clarified in the notebook that the regime table is ex-post diagnostics.
The named regimes are known historical event windows; the `picked_strategy`
column is not a live switching rule. The live-style switch in this research is
the multi-condition opportunity filter based on expanding prediction, curve, and
momentum dispersion thresholds.

Testability of next ideas
-------------------------
Adding soybean meal/oil futures is not testable with the current train_set
alone. It requires meal and oil futures price histories, contract multipliers,
leg ratios, and leg-level costs. Once those are available, the crush spread
should be treated as a tradable instrument and tested on the extended holdout.

Pre-registering the multi-condition filter is testable now as a written rule:
freeze prediction/curve/momentum dispersion thresholds at q=0.40/0.40/0.40,
use expanding lagged thresholds only, and do not change them after seeing the
2021-February 2026 holdout. The notebook now states this explicitly.

Overfit warning on hard thresholds
----------------------------------
The q=0.40/0.40/0.40 multi-condition filter can still be overfit because it was
selected after looking at this sample and validation window. I added two more
natural regime-weighting checks: continuous opportunity-weighted exposure and a
lagged trailing-performance ensemble. They avoid named historical regime labels
and hard cutoffs, but they underperformed in the current data. They should be
kept as holdout robustness checks, not promoted yet.
