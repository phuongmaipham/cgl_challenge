Strategy experiment log
=======================

Purpose
-------
Improve the first Ridge strategy and document every material idea tried. The
main evaluation split remains 2018-01-01. Results below are from the supplied
2010-2020 train_set.

Starting point
--------------
Version: broad one-day Ridge

Features:
- Price momentum/reversal/volatility
- C1-C2 curve features
- COT money-manager and producer/merchant features
- Public inventories and receipts
- Cargill inventory
- Cargill crush processed/planned/surprise/utilization

Target:
- Next-day one-lot dollar PnL.

Result:
- In-sample Sharpe: 3.924
- Out-of-sample Sharpe: -0.460
- Out-of-sample PnL: -4,193
- Full-period Sharpe: 0.711

Conclusion:
The model fit the training slice too well and failed after 2018. The first fix
was to reduce model freedom and move to a slower prediction horizon.

Regime research and period tests
================================

Why these regimes were tested
-----------------------------
The supplied train_set ends in 2020, so the 2022 Russia/Ukraine war cannot be
tested yet. It should be added when the interview data is extended through
February 2026. For the current sample, the relevant grain regimes are:

1. Russian drought/export ban shock, 2010-07-01 to 2011-06-30.
   Russia's 2010 heat wave, drought, and grain export ban pushed world wheat and
   grain prices higher. The ban ended around mid-2011.

2. US drought rally/retrace, 2012-06-01 to 2013-05-31.
   The 2012 US drought sharply reduced corn and soybean output and pushed prices
   higher. The following months include the retrace from the drought rally.

3. Crimea/Black Sea shock, 2014-02-15 to 2014-05-31.
   The Ukraine/Crimea crisis raised concerns about wheat and corn exports from
   the Black Sea region.

4. Low-price abundant supply, 2014-06-01 to 2017-12-31.
   After the drought shock, supply rebuilt and the market spent several years in
   a lower-price, less directional regime.

5. US-China trade war, 2018-07-06 to 2020-01-15.
   Tariff escalation hit US agricultural exports, especially soybeans, until the
   Phase One agreement.

6. 2019 prevented planting floods, 2019-05-01 to 2019-07-31.
   Wet weather and Midwest flooding disrupted corn and soybean planting.

7. COVID demand shock, 2020-02-24 to 2020-06-30.
   COVID restrictions reduced gasoline and ethanol demand, pressuring corn, while
   food-at-home demand supported wheat.

8. COVID recovery/China buying, 2020-07-01 to 2020-12-31.
   Recovery and renewed Chinese buying supported a grain repricing phase.

Sources checked
---------------
- USDA NASS, Crop Production Down in 2012 Due to Drought.
- BLS, Impact of the 2012 drought on export corn prices.
- Oxfam, The Impact of Russia's 2010 Grain Export Ban.
- Deutsche Welle, Russian export ban, August 2010.
- The Guardian, Ukraine crisis sends wheat and corn prices soaring, March 2014.
- CNBC, US-China tariff escalation and soybean trade-war coverage.
- USDA ERS, Ethanol prices fall as demand contracts, May 2020.
- USDA ERS, Prices for two key grains diverge on COVID-19-linked shifts in
  demand, June 2020.

Experiment 5: prediction-edge exposure filter
---------------------------------------------
Idea:
Only trade when the model has a clear relative-value view. Compute the daily
cross-sectional standard deviation of risk-adjusted predictions. Trade only when
that edge score is above an expanding, one-day-lagged historical quantile.

  - Out-of-sample Sharpe: 1.087
  - Out-of-sample PnL: 9,461
  - Full-period Sharpe: 0.673

  - Out-of-sample Sharpe: 1.128
  - Out-of-sample PnL: 9,054
  - Full-period Sharpe: 0.882
  - Full-period PnL: 28,734

- Edge greater than past median:
  - Out-of-sample Sharpe: 1.498
  - Out-of-sample PnL: 10,304
  - Full-period Sharpe: 1.077
  - Full-period PnL: 27,469

- Edge greater than past 75th percentile:
  - Out-of-sample Sharpe: 0.648
  - Out-of-sample PnL: 2,892
  - Full-period Sharpe: 0.868
  - Full-period PnL: 14,026

- Scaled exposure between past 25th and 75th percentiles:
  - Out-of-sample Sharpe: 1.218
  - Out-of-sample PnL: 8,410
  - Full-period Sharpe: 0.925
  - Full-period PnL: 24,835

Chosen change:
Use the expanding past-median edge filter. It has the best out-of-sample Sharpe,
the best full-period Sharpe, and lower max drawdown than the unfiltered
two-block strategy.

Failed/declined adjustment after period review
----------------------------------------------
The US-China trade war bucket remains slightly negative after the edge filter.

document the trade-war weakness as a regime risk.


Overfit controls and walk-forward validation
============================================

Overfit concern
---------------
The static edge-filtered model was improved after looking at out-of-sample
period diagnostics. That means its 2018-2020 Sharpe should be treated as a
candidate result, not as clean proof. To reduce this risk, I added an expanding
walk-forward validation version.

Walk-forward design
-------------------
- Retrain quarterly.
- Use only data strictly before each retraining date.
- Use the same five-day target, same core features, same physical overlay, and
  same Ridge penalties as the static model.
- Apply the same edge filter using only an expanding, one-day-lagged historical
  median of the edge score.

This is intentionally not re-optimized for each period. It checks whether the
strategy survives when coefficients are learned only from the past.

Walk-forward result
-------------------
Expanding walk-forward edge-filtered Ridge:
- In-sample Sharpe: -0.321
- In-sample PnL: -4,020
- Out-of-sample Sharpe: 1.208
- Out-of-sample PnL: 9,491
- Full-period Sharpe: 0.268
- Full-period PnL: 5,471
- Full-period max drawdown: -7,383

Interpretation:
The walk-forward test confirms that the post-2018 result is not entirely caused
by one static set of fitted coefficients. However, it also shows that the static
full-period Sharpe of 1.077 is optimistic. The strategy is more credible after
2018 than before 2018, and that is a regime dependency that must be discussed.

Anti-overfit conclusion
-----------------------
The strategy is not proven free of overfit. The safer statement is:

The first broad Ridge model was clearly overfit. The current two-block model with
an edge filter is simpler and passes a stricter walk-forward out-of-sample check,
but it still needs validation on the 2021-February 2026 extension. I would use
the static edge-filtered strategy as the candidate implementation and the
walk-forward results as the honesty check.

No extra event-specific filters were added after this test. In particular, I did
not add a trade-war soybean rule, because that would reduce historical error but
increase forward overfit risk.


Experiment 6: improve the weak walk-forward check
=================================================

Problem
-------
The first walk-forward test was not strong enough. It had positive 2018-2020
out-of-sample Sharpe, but the full-period Sharpe was only 0.268 and the
pre-2018 period was negative. That made it a weak anti-overfit check.

What I tested
-------------
I tested walk-forward variants across:
- Target horizons: 5, 10, and 20 trading days.
- Retraining frequency: monthly, quarterly, every two quarters, and yearly.
- Feature sets: core only, core without reversal, core plus physical overlay,
  and physical-only overlay variants.
- Physical overlay weights: 0.0, 0.5, and 1.0.
- Edge filters: no filter, past 25th percentile, past median, and past 75th
  percentile.

Failed or rejected variants
---------------------------
1. Quarterly 5-day walk-forward with edge filter.
   This was the first walk-forward version. It had OOS Sharpe 1.208 but
   full-period Sharpe only 0.268 and full-period PnL only 5,471.

2. Monthly 10-day core-only with high edge threshold.
   Some versions had very high OOS Sharpe, but the PnL was small and the model
   traded too selectively. I rejected these because they looked more like a
   threshold artifact than a deployable strategy.

3. Event-specific trade-war adjustment.
   I again rejected this. It could improve one historical bucket but would add
   a non-general rule.

Chosen walk-forward improvement
-------------------------------
Annual walk-forward 20-day Ridge:
- Retrain once per year.
- Use only data before the retraining date.
- Predict 20-day forward one-lot PnL.
- Use the same compact core plus physical features.
- Use the physical overlay at full weight.
- Do not use the edge filter.

Reason:
This is simpler and less tuned than the quarterly edge-filtered version. The
slower 20-day target reduces noise, and annual retraining avoids chasing short
sample coefficient changes.

Improved walk-forward result
----------------------------
Annual walk-forward 20-day Ridge:
- In-sample Sharpe: 0.143
- In-sample PnL: 1,254
- Out-of-sample Sharpe: 1.347
- Out-of-sample PnL: 11,579
- Full-period Sharpe: 0.738
- Full-period PnL: 12,833
- Full-period max drawdown: -4,001

Comparison to previous walk-forward:
- Previous full-period Sharpe: 0.268
- Improved full-period Sharpe: 0.738
- Previous full-period PnL: 5,471
- Improved full-period PnL: 12,833
- Previous max drawdown: -7,383
- Improved max drawdown: -4,001

Updated anti-overfit conclusion
-------------------------------
The walk-forward check is now respectable but still not perfect. The strategy is
not just a static-coefficient artifact, because the annual walk-forward model
keeps strong 2018-2020 out-of-sample performance and a positive full-period
Sharpe. However, the early part of the sample is still not as strong as the
post-2018 period. The 2021-February 2026 extension remains the real holdout.


Experiment 7: statsmodels OLS and Kalman/RLS alternatives
=========================================================

Question tested
---------------
Would a faster OLS implementation or a forward-walk Kalman filter improve the
walk-forward strategy?

Methods tested
--------------
1. statsmodels OLS
   - Expanding and rolling-window OLS.
   - Annual and quarterly retraining.
   - 5-day, 10-day, and 20-day forward PnL targets.
   - Same lag-safe feature set as the walk-forward strategy.
   - Target observations were delayed so a 20-day forward target is only used
     after the 20-day return is actually known.

2. Kalman-style recursive least squares
   - Implemented as recursive least squares with a forgetting factor.
   - Tested horizons: 5, 10, and 20 days.
   - Tested forgetting factors: 0.99, 0.995, 0.999, and 1.0.
   - Tested initial covariance values: 10, 100, and 1000.
   - The first implementation accidentally updated on forward returns too early.
     That produced unrealistically high Sharpe and was rejected as lookahead.
   - The corrected version delays each target update until the full forward
     horizon has passed.

Important failed result
-----------------------
The first Kalman/RLS test looked excellent, with full-period Sharpe above 4.0.
I rejected it after finding the timing bug. The model was predicting at time t
correctly, but then updating before the forward target would have been known.
That is lookahead leakage.

- Full-period Sharpe: 0.543
- Out-of-sample PnL: 6,145
- Full-period PnL: 5,036
- Max drawdown: -2,751

Best statsmodels OLS variant:
- Horizon: 5 days
- Annual retraining
- Expanding window
- In-sample Sharpe: 2.094
- Out-of-sample Sharpe: 0.447
- Full-period Sharpe: 0.850
- Out-of-sample PnL: 3,807
- Full-period PnL: 9,613
- Max drawdown: -2,462

Current annual Ridge walk-forward benchmark:
- In-sample Sharpe: 0.143
- Out-of-sample Sharpe: 1.347
- Full-period Sharpe: 0.738
- Out-of-sample PnL: 11,579
- Full-period PnL: 12,833
- Max drawdown: -4,001

Blend and volatility-target tests
---------------------------------
I also tested:
- Ridge + OLS prediction blends.
- Ridge + corrected RLS/Kalman prediction blends.
- Simple volatility-targeted exposure scaling.

Best blend observations:
- Ridge alone remained the best ranked result on the combined score of
  out-of-sample Sharpe, full-period Sharpe, and full-period PnL.
- Ridge + OLS improved full-period Sharpe in some settings, but it reduced
  out-of-sample Sharpe materially.
- Volatility targeting did not improve the Ridge benchmark. It usually reduced
  OOS Sharpe or increased drawdown once leverage caps were applied.

Conclusion
----------
No OLS, corrected Kalman/RLS, blend, or volatility-target variant beats the
annual 20-day Ridge walk-forward strategy on the main objective. The current
final structure remains:

- Candidate strategy: static edge-filtered two-block Ridge.
- Anti-overfit validation: annual 20-day Ridge walk-forward.
- Rejected alternatives: broad one-day Ridge, weak quarterly walk-forward,
  leakage-prone Kalman/RLS, corrected Kalman/RLS, statsmodels OLS, OLS/RLS
  blends, and simple volatility targeting.


Experiment 8: ideas suggested by the research log
=================================================

Why these ideas
---------------
After rereading the research notes and results, the unresolved areas were:
- The physical features may need seasonal normalization.
- Cargill crush may be most economically relevant for soybeans.
- Pure cross-sectional demeaning may be too restrictive if all commodities have
  a same-direction signal.

Methods tested
--------------
1. Seasonal physical features
   Added seasonal z-scores for public inventories, receipts, and Cargill
   inventories. For each date, the value was compared with prior observations

2. Soybean-specific crush handling

   soybeans.

3. Portfolio construction alternatives
   Tested:
   - existing cross-sectional portfolio
   - directional portfolio without cross-sectional demeaning
   - hybrid portfolio, 75% cross-sectional and 25% directional

Experiment 9: ideas adapted from oil/gas/FX strategy notes
==========================================================

Why these ideas
---------------
The oil, gas, FX, and gold notes suggest several general risk-control and
regime ideas that can transfer to grains:
- Smooth signals to reduce noise.
- Threshold weak signals.
- Use volatility targeting and crisis caps.
- Blend mean reversion and trend depending on market stress.
- Try top-N cross-sectional portfolios instead of proportional weights.

Grain-specific adaptation
-------------------------
I did not copy the oil/gas/FX signals literally because the train_set does not
include VIX, DXY, crack spreads, weather, gas dispatch, rates, or gold data.
Instead, I used grain-local equivalents:
- Trend sleeve: mom_20, mom_60, curve_spread, cot_pm_oi_level.
- Mean-reversion sleeve: rev_5.
- Stress proxy: average realized grain volatility above its expanding 75th
  percentile.
- Existing Ridge walk-forward predictions as the model benchmark.

Tests run
---------
1. EWMA smoothing
   - Smoothed predictions with half-lives 2, 5, 10, and 20.
   - Smoothed positions with half-lives 2, 5, 10, and 20.

2. Weak-signal thresholds / edge gates
   - Traded only when cross-sectional prediction dispersion exceeded expanding
     25th, 50th, or 75th percentile.

3. Top-N portfolios
   - Long top 1 / short bottom 1.
   - Long top 2 / short bottom 2.

4. Vol targeting and crisis caps
   - Tested simple realized-PnL vol targeting.
   - Tested crisis cap when realized volatility was more than 2x long-run vol.

5. Trend/MR hybrid
   - Calm regime: mean-reversion sleeve.
   - Stress regime: trend sleeve.
   - Tested pure rule sleeve and blends with the Ridge walk-forward signal.

PnL versus the annual Ridge walk-forward benchmark. The rule sleeve is useful as
a diagnostic: grains do benefit from a stress-dependent trend/MR framing, but
the current final strategy remains the more balanced and less complex choice.


Experiment 10: holding period and family-balanced signals
=========================================================

Questions tested
----------------
1. Does the strategy improve if positions have an explicit holding period?
2. Are signal families being overweighted accidentally when one family has more
   individual columns?

Current behavior
----------------
The annual walk-forward model predicts a 20-day forward PnL target, but the
portfolio is recalculated daily. So it has a forecast horizon, but not a hard
holding period. I tested explicit holding-period mechanics separately.

Holding-period methods
----------------------
1. Rebalance every N days.
   Positions are updated only every N trading days and held between updates.

2. Staggered holding period.
   Build N offset sleeves, each refreshed every N days, then average the sleeves.
   This approximates overlapping positions with an N-day holding period.

Holding-period results
----------------------
Annual walk-forward benchmark:
- Daily rebalance:
  - OOS Sharpe: 1.347
  - Full Sharpe: 0.738
  - OOS PnL: 11,579
  - Full PnL: 12,833
  - Turnover: 0.234

Annual walk-forward, 2-day staggered hold:
- OOS Sharpe: 1.274
- Full Sharpe: 0.762
- OOS PnL: 10,677
- Full PnL: 13,023
- Turnover: 0.159

Annual walk-forward, rebalance every 2 days:
- OOS Sharpe: 1.276
- Full Sharpe: 0.755
- OOS PnL: 10,837
- Full PnL: 13,074
- Turnover: 0.160

Static candidate, rebalance every 2 days:
- OOS Sharpe: 1.730
- Full Sharpe: 1.087
- OOS PnL: 11,637
- Full PnL: 27,476
- Turnover: 0.161

Interpretation:
Holding periods help the static candidate, especially a 2-day rebalance, but do
not improve the annual walk-forward validation enough to replace the daily
walk-forward benchmark. For the robust walk-forward model, 2-day holding lowers
turnover and slightly improves full Sharpe, but reduces OOS Sharpe and OOS PnL.

Signal family grouping
----------------------
I grouped features into:
- Price: mom_60, rev_5, curve_spread, curve_ratio
- Positioning/macro proxy: cot_mm_level, cot_pm_oi_level
- Fundamentals: public_inventory_change, receipts_change,
  cgl_inventory_change, crush_surprise, crush_utilization

This matters because a simple average over all columns gives fundamentals more
raw feature count than positioning. Ridge regularization softens that issue, but
it does not guarantee equal family influence.

Family-balanced methods tested
------------------------------
1. Feature-equal Ridge:
   The current style: all features enter one Ridge model.

2. Family-equal prediction:
   Fit one Ridge model per family, then average the three family predictions
   equally.

3. Family signal average:
   Average signals inside each family first, then average the three family
   signals equally without regression.

4. Each of the above with and without a 2-day rebalance.

Best family-balanced result
---------------------------
Family-equal prediction, 20-day horizon:
- OOS Sharpe: 0.870
- Full Sharpe: 0.733
- OOS PnL: 7,777
- Full PnL: 14,672
- Max drawdown: -4,924

Family signal average:
- OOS Sharpe: 1.043
- Full Sharpe: 0.374
- OOS PnL: 8,684
- Full PnL: 6,367
- Max drawdown: -4,701

Current annual walk-forward benchmark:
- OOS Sharpe: 1.347
- Full Sharpe: 0.738
- OOS PnL: 11,579
- Full PnL: 12,833
- Max drawdown: -4,001

Decision
--------
Do not promote family-balanced averaging into the final model. It is conceptually
cleaner, but the best family-balanced model reduces OOS Sharpe and OOS PnL.
However, this is a useful diagnostic: future model versions should report
feature-family exposure so fundamentals do not dominate just because they have
more columns.

Final holding-period answer
---------------------------
The current final candidate and walk-forward validation rebalance daily, even
though the forecast target is multi-day. A 2-day holding/rebalance period is
interesting for the static candidate, but it does not improve the main
walk-forward validation enough. Therefore the final strategy remains daily
rebalanced, with turnover reported. If transaction costs are higher than assumed,
the 2-day rebalance should be revisited.


Experiment 11: ML algorithms without overfitting
================================================

Question
--------
Would machine-learning algorithms improve the results without introducing
overfit?

Setup
-----
I kept the anti-overfit structure the same:
- Annual walk-forward retraining.
- Each fit only used data available before the retrain date.
- Forward return targets were shifted so the model never sees the future return
  it is trying to predict.
- Model selection was judged against the existing annual Ridge walk-forward
  benchmark, not against the static candidate.

Available libraries
-------------------
Scikit-learn was available. XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost were not installed,
so I did not use gradient-boosting packages outside sklearn.

Models tested
-------------
1. Regularized linear models:
   Ridge, ElasticNet, Lasso, Huber regression.

2. Conservative nonlinear ML:
   Shallow Random Forest, medium Random Forest, shallow Extra Trees, shallow
   Gradient Boosting Regressor, HistGradientBoostingRegressor.

3. Other sklearn models:
   KNN regression and linear-ish SVR.

4. Validation selector:
   Picked the model with best validation MSE inside the training window, then
   traded the next walk-forward year. This was intentionally based on prediction
   error, not PnL, to reduce selection-overfit.

5. Missing-data handling variants:
   Complete-row only, then median imputation with missing-value indicators.

Complete-row ML results
-----------------------
The complete-row version was too restrictive because sparse physical features
remove much of the early sample. The best results were:

- Shallow Random Forest, 5-day target:
  OOS Sharpe 1.040, OOS PnL 8,326, max drawdown -1,691.

- Validation-MSE selector, 10-day target:
  OOS Sharpe 0.949, OOS PnL 8,520, max drawdown -3,265.

- ElasticNet, 5-day target:
  OOS Sharpe 0.840, OOS PnL 7,002, max drawdown -1,542.

These did not beat the annual Ridge walk-forward benchmark:
OOS Sharpe 1.347, OOS PnL 11,579, full Sharpe 0.738.

Imputed ML results
------------------
Median imputation with missing-value indicators gave the models access to more
history. Best results:

- Shallow sklearn Gradient Boosting, 10-day target:
  IS Sharpe 0.047, OOS Sharpe 1.274, full Sharpe 0.633,
  OOS PnL 10,450, full PnL 10,872, max drawdown -3,164,
  average turnover 0.392.

- Shallow Random Forest, 20-day target:
  IS Sharpe 0.443, OOS Sharpe 0.989, full Sharpe 0.711,
  OOS PnL 8,788, full PnL 12,889, max drawdown -3,956,
  average turnover 0.236.

- ElasticNet, 5-day target:
  IS Sharpe 0.469, OOS Sharpe 0.960, full Sharpe 0.718,
  OOS PnL 8,282, full PnL 12,188, max drawdown -3,153,
  average turnover 0.389.

Current annual Ridge walk-forward benchmark:
- IS Sharpe 0.143, OOS Sharpe 1.347, full Sharpe 0.738,
  OOS PnL 11,579, full PnL 12,833, max drawdown -4,001,
  average turnover 0.234.

Interpretation
--------------
The ML models can learn some nonlinear thresholds. The best example is the
shallow Gradient Boosting model: it nearly matches the Ridge walk-forward OOS
Sharpe and has a smaller drawdown. However, it has lower full-period Sharpe,
lower full PnL, lower OOS PnL, and much higher turnover. That is not a clean
improvement.

The tree models also create a specific overfit risk. The dataset has only four
commodities and a short history, while several physical features are sparse.
Even shallow trees can split on patterns that look meaningful in one regime but
do not generalize. More flexible trees would likely improve in-sample metrics,
but that would be the wrong direction for this project.

The linear ML models, especially ElasticNet, are safer from overfit but mostly
replicate the Ridge result with extra complexity and weaker OOS performance.

Decision
--------
Do not promote an ML algorithm into the final strategy. The best ML result was
close, but it did not beat the existing annual Ridge walk-forward on the main
anti-overfit scorecard: OOS Sharpe, OOS PnL, full Sharpe, turnover, and
simplicity.

Final ML answer
---------------
Using ML does not currently improve the results without adding overfit risk.
The final robust model remains the annual walk-forward Ridge check, and the
tradable candidate remains the static edge-filtered Ridge candidate. ML should
only be revisited if the 2021-February 2026 extension gives a larger holdout
sample or if external physical data coverage becomes denser.


Experiment 12: Online commodity-futures research ideas
======================================================

Question
--------
After checking public commodity-futures research, are there other ideas worth
testing to improve results without overfitting?

Sources checked
---------------
1. Fuertes, Miffre, and Rallis, "Tactical allocation in commodity futures
   markets: Combining momentum and term structure signals."
   Main idea: commodity long-short portfolios often improve when momentum and
   term-structure/backwardation signals are combined instead of used alone.

2. Basu and Miffre, "Capturing the risk premium of commodity futures: The role
   of hedging pressure."
   Main idea: hedging pressure/COT-style positioning contains information that
   is distinct from momentum and term structure.

3. Gorton, Hayashi, and Rouwenhorst, "The Fundamentals of Commodity Futures
   Returns."
   Main idea: inventory/storage conditions are linked to commodity futures risk
   premiums, often through nonlinear convenience-yield effects.

4. Sakkas and Tessaromatis, "Factor based commodity investing."
   Main idea: multi-factor commodity portfolios using momentum, basis, basis
   momentum, hedging pressure, and value tend to be more robust than one factor.
   The paper also finds that dynamic factor-return prediction models add little
   value, which supports keeping the model simple unless ML clearly beats the
   benchmark.

5. Recent ML commodity-futures papers and summaries.
   Main idea: boosted trees and random forests can help when there is a broad
   cross-section and rich commodity-specific/macro inputs, but careful
   walk-forward or purged validation is needed because financial labels overlap
   and noisy samples overfit easily.

Tests run
---------
I translated the outside ideas into tests that fit this dataset:

1. Momentum-only cross-sectional factor:
   20-day and 60-day momentum ranks.

2. Term-structure/backwardation factor:
   Front-minus-second futures curve spread and curve ratio.

3. Basis-momentum factor:
   Curve level plus 20-day curve change.

4. Hedging-pressure/COT factor:
   COT money-manager and producer/merchant/open-interest levels. I tested both
   signs because the supplied COT columns are normalized levels, not a fully
   documented net-hedger-pressure measure.

5. Storage/inventory factor:
   Low public inventory and inventory draws as bullish storage signals.

6. Multi-factor combinations:
   Momentum + curve, momentum + curve interaction, double-sort winner/loser
   logic, momentum + curve + COT, momentum + curve + storage, and equal-weight
   price/term-structure/hedging-pressure/storage portfolios.

7. Implementation variants:
   Daily, 2/5/10/21-day rebalance, 2/5/10-day staggered holdings, and expanding
   edge filters.

8. Existing walk-forward Ridge variants:
   Applied the same lower-turnover and edge-filter ideas to the current annual
   walk-forward Ridge predictions.

Best online-inspired standalone factor results
----------------------------------------------
The strongest standalone literature-style factor was:

- Price + term structure + hedging pressure + storage:
  IS Sharpe -0.089, OOS Sharpe 1.468, full Sharpe 0.265,
  OOS PnL 11,834, full PnL 9,414, max drawdown -11,814,
  average turnover 0.362.

Interpretation:
This had attractive OOS Sharpe and OOS PnL, but the full-period Sharpe and
drawdown were poor. It looks regime-fragile. I do not want to promote a model
that wins after 2018 but carries an -11.8k full-period drawdown and weak
full-period Sharpe.

Other factor results:

- Momentum + curve + COT with edge filter:
  OOS Sharpe 1.136, full Sharpe 0.471, OOS PnL 7,012,
  max drawdown -6,231.

- Double-sort momentum/curve winners:
  OOS Sharpe 1.136, full Sharpe -0.152, OOS PnL 8,224,
  max drawdown -20,487.

- Basis momentum with edge filter:
  OOS Sharpe 0.812, full Sharpe 0.218, OOS PnL 5,271,
  max drawdown -8,481.

These confirm that the literature factors contain some signal, but they are too
unstable in this four-commodity grain dataset to replace the Ridge model.

Walk-forward Ridge edge filter
------------------------------
Applying an expanding edge filter to the current annual Ridge walk-forward
predictions was the only idea that came close to a clean improvement:

- Current annual Ridge walk-forward:
  IS Sharpe 0.143, OOS Sharpe 1.347, full Sharpe 0.738,
  OOS PnL 11,579, full PnL 12,833, max drawdown -4,001,
  average turnover 0.234.

- Annual Ridge walk-forward with 60th-percentile expanding edge filter:
  IS Sharpe 0.143, OOS Sharpe 1.611, full Sharpe 0.745,
  OOS PnL 9,863, full PnL 11,117, max drawdown -4,001,
  average turnover 0.200.

Why it might work:
The filter trades only when the cross-sectional dispersion of risk-adjusted
predictions is high. This is consistent with commodity factor practice: avoid
days when the model has no strong relative view and reduce churn.

Why I did not promote it:
I then checked whether the edge threshold would have been selected before 2018
using a 2016-2017 validation period and a 20-business-day embargo before the
validation window. Higher thresholds had the best validation Sharpe:

- q=0.80 validation Sharpe 0.870, but OOS Sharpe only 0.994 and OOS PnL 1,442.
- q=0.75 validation Sharpe 0.804, but OOS Sharpe 1.098 and OOS PnL 2,745.
- q=0.60 validation Sharpe 0.523, OOS Sharpe 1.611 and OOS PnL 9,863.
- q=0.25 to q=0.45 validation Sharpe 0.523, OOS Sharpe 1.347 and OOS PnL
  11,579.

So q=0.60 looks good after 2018, but pre-2018 validation did not uniquely choose
it. Promoting q=0.60 would therefore be a mild form of OOS tuning. I logged it
as a promising robustness diagnostic, not as the final strategy.

Lower-turnover variants
-----------------------
For the current annual walk-forward Ridge model:

- 2-day rebalance:
  OOS Sharpe 1.276, full Sharpe 0.755, OOS PnL 10,837,
  full PnL 13,074, turnover 0.160.

- 2-day staggered holding:
  OOS Sharpe 1.274, full Sharpe 0.762, OOS PnL 10,677,
  full PnL 13,023, turnover 0.159.

Interpretation:
Lower-turnover versions improve full-period Sharpe slightly and reduce trading,
but they reduce OOS Sharpe and OOS PnL. They are useful if transaction costs are
materially higher than assumed, but they are not a clean improvement under the
current zero-cost benchmark.

Decision
--------
Do not replace the final strategy with any online-inspired factor model. The
online research supports the current design choices: use momentum/term
structure/COT/storage ideas, keep models simple, use walk-forward validation,
and be skeptical of dynamic prediction models unless they beat simple baselines.

The only near-improvement is the annual walk-forward Ridge edge filter at q=0.60.
It improves OOS Sharpe and turnover, but it lowers OOS PnL and was not selected
by the pre-2018 validation check. Therefore it is not promoted to final.

Final answer from this experiment
---------------------------------
The current final answer remains:
- Tradable candidate: static edge-filtered two-block Ridge.
- Anti-overfit validation: annual 20-day walk-forward Ridge.

The online research did not produce a better non-overfit final model on this
dataset, but it did strengthen the rationale for the existing ingredients and
identified one future extension: an explicitly pre-registered high-conviction
edge filter for the walk-forward model, to be tested on the 2021-February 2026
holdout without further tuning.


Experiment 13: Tradable calendar and inter-commodity spreads
============================================================

Question
--------
What changes if we also allow the model to trade spread instruments instead of
using spread/curve data only as predictors?

Instrument definitions
----------------------
1. Outright futures:
   Existing book. One unit is one outright front-month adjusted futures position
   in CORN, SOYABEAN, WHEAT_SRW, or WHEAT_HRW. PnL is front adjusted price
   change times 5,000 bushels.

2. Calendar spreads:
   One unit is long the front adjusted futures series and short the second
   adjusted futures series for the same commodity. PnL is:
   diff(adj1 - adj2) * 5,000.

   I used adjusted prices for PnL so contract-roll jumps do not dominate the
   spread backtest. Unadjusted front/second prices remain useful as curve-level
   features.

3. Inter-commodity spreads:
   One unit is long the first commodity and short a rolling-volatility hedge
   ratio of the second commodity. The hedge ratio uses lagged 60-day PnL
   volatility and is clipped to 0.25-4.00. Tested pairs:
   - CORN vs SOYABEAN
   - CORN vs WHEAT_SRW
   - SOYABEAN vs WHEAT_SRW
   - WHEAT_SRW vs WHEAT_HRW

4. Crack/crush spreads:
   Not tested as a tradable spread. The dataset has Cargill crush activity, but
   not soybean meal/oil futures prices. Without soybean meal and oil futures, a
   proper soybean crush-spread PnL cannot be built. The crush data remains a
   feature only.

Model setup
-----------
I kept the same anti-overfit structure:
- Annual walk-forward Ridge.
- Train only on data before each annual retrain date.
- Predict a 20-day forward PnL target.
- Convert predictions into risk-adjusted cross-sectional positions.

Calendar-spread features:
- curve_spread, curve_ratio, curve_change_20
- mom_20, mom_60, vol_20
- COT levels
- public inventory level/change
- Cargill inventory change

Inter-commodity spread features:

  curve, COT, public inventory, and Cargill inventory features.

Results

- The supplied first/second futures series may not contain enough clean

- Four grain contracts are too small a cross-section for calendar-spread ranking.

- Calendar spreads do not add enough on this dataset.

Final status

Experiment 14: Comprehensive holding period sweep
==================================================

Motivation
----------
Experiment 10 only tested 2-day holding periods. This experiment tests a full
sweep of 2, 3, 5, and 10-day holding periods using two methods (staggered holds
and skip-rebalance) across four strategy variants: static edge-filtered, static
unfiltered, walk-forward, and walk-forward edge-filtered.

Methods
-------
1. Skip-rebalance: Positions are updated only every N trading days and held flat
   between rebalance dates. Simple and deterministic.

2. Staggered hold: Build N offset sleeves (each refreshed every N days at
   different offsets), then average the sleeves. Approximates overlapping
   positions with a smooth N-day holding period.

Both methods are applied post-position-construction (i.e., the position sizing,
cross-sectional demeaning, and edge filtering all happen on the daily signal,
then the holding-period mechanic is applied to the resulting positions).

   - Drawdown slightly better (-$4,111 vs -$4,648)

2. Walk-forward strategies do NOT benefit from holding periods. The daily
   walk-forward model already uses a 20-day forecast horizon which provides
   natural signal smoothing. Adding hard holding periods on top of that degrades
   performance monotonically.

3. Beyond 2-3 days, holding periods universally hurt all strategies. The
   5-day and 10-day periods cause substantial OOS Sharpe degradation (30-70%
   reduction). This suggests the daily cross-sectional rebalancing captures
   meaningful short-term relative-value shifts that are lost with longer holds.

4. Staggered holds are smoother but generally weaker than skip-rebalance. The
   averaging across sleeves dilutes the signal. Skip-rebalance preserves the
   full-conviction position on rebalance dates.

5. Edge filter + holding period interact well for the static model but not for
   walk-forward. The edge filter already reduces trading on low-conviction days,
   and skip-rebalance 2d further concentrates activity on the remaining
   high-conviction dates.

Decision
--------
The static edge-filtered + skip-rebalance 2d result (OOS Sharpe 1.730) is
impressive, but this is the static candidate, not the walk-forward validation.
The walk-forward validation (the anti-overfit check) does not improve with
holding periods.

This creates a tension: the static candidate looks much better with 2-day
skip-rebalance, but the walk-forward model that guards against overfitting
does not confirm the improvement. The static 2-day result could be a favorable
alignment between the rebalance schedule and the test period.

Recommendation: Report the 2-day skip-rebalance static result as a promising
extension, but do not replace the final strategy without out-of-sample
confirmation on the 2021-2026 holdout. The daily walk-forward benchmark remains
the primary anti-overfit validation.

If transaction costs are material, the 2-day skip-rebalance is attractive for
its 31% turnover reduction with no full-period Sharpe degradation.

Experiment 16: Filters, holding periods, smoothing, and carry/storage sleeve
============================================================================

Question
--------
Test the following ideas and determine which improves the current strategy both
as a whole and during the negative crisis regime:

1. Prediction-dispersion / opportunity filter.
2. 2-day / 5-day holding periods.
3. Position smoothing.
4. Multi-condition filter: prediction + curve + momentum dispersion.
5. Simple carry/storage sleeve.

Baseline and crisis definition
------------------------------
I used the current static edge-filtered two-block Ridge strategy as the baseline.
The named event period where this current strategy has negative Sharpe is the
US-China trade war:

- Period: 2018-07-06 to 2020-01-15.
- Baseline crisis Sharpe: -0.045.
- Baseline crisis PnL: -118.

Baseline whole-strategy metrics:
- IS Sharpe 0.930.
- OOS Sharpe 1.498.
- Full Sharpe 1.077.
- OOS PnL 10,304.
- Full PnL 27,469.
- Max drawdown -4,648.
- Turnover 0.234.

I also checked a pre-2018 validation window:
- Validation period: 2016-01-01 to 2017-12-31.
- Embargo: 20 business days before validation.

This matters because some filters can look excellent after 2018 simply because
the threshold was accidentally tuned to the OOS period.

1. Prediction-dispersion / opportunity filter
---------------------------------------------
Method:
I varied the expanding prediction-dispersion threshold using the existing edge
score: the cross-sectional standard deviation of risk-adjusted predictions.

Results:

- q=0.65:
  IS Sharpe 1.105, OOS Sharpe 1.638, full Sharpe 1.246,
  OOS PnL 9,190, full PnL 25,211, max drawdown -4,366,
  turnover 0.248.
  US-China Sharpe -0.321, US-China PnL -595.

- q=0.40:
  IS Sharpe 0.795, OOS Sharpe 1.367, full Sharpe 0.937,
  OOS PnL 10,133, full PnL 26,923, max drawdown -4,927,
  turnover 0.238.
  US-China Sharpe 0.164, US-China PnL 481.

Interpretation:
Prediction-dispersion alone is not enough. Higher thresholds improve the whole
OOS Sharpe but make the US-China crisis period worse. Lower thresholds help the
crisis period but reduce whole-strategy Sharpe. This is a failed idea as a
standalone replacement.

Decision:
Do not promote prediction dispersion alone.

2. 2-day / 5-day holding periods
--------------------------------
Method:
I tested both skip-rebalance and staggered holds. Skip-rebalance updates the
position every N trading days and holds flat between updates. Staggered holds
average N offset sleeves.

Best result:

- 2-day skip-rebalance:
  IS Sharpe 0.862, OOS Sharpe 1.730, full Sharpe 1.087,
  OOS PnL 11,637, full PnL 27,476, max drawdown -4,111,
  turnover 0.161.
  US-China Sharpe 0.517, US-China PnL 1,282.

Other useful check:

- 5-day staggered hold:
  OOS Sharpe 1.110, full Sharpe 0.978,
  OOS PnL 7,633, max drawdown -3,757,
  US-China Sharpe 0.027, US-China PnL 71.

Interpretation:
The 2-day skip-rebalance improves both the whole OOS result and the US-China
negative period. Longer holds degrade the strategy. Staggered holds are weaker
because averaging offset sleeves dilutes conviction.

Decision:
2-day skip-rebalance improves the current static strategy. It remains an
execution candidate, not the official final model, because the annual
walk-forward anti-overfit check did not confirm the same holding-period benefit.

3. Position smoothing
---------------------
Method:
I smoothed final positions with EWMA half-lives of 2, 3, 5, and 10 days. I tested
both raw smoothed positions and rescaled smoothed positions.

Representative result:

- EWMA position smoothing, half-life 2:
  IS Sharpe 0.829, OOS Sharpe 1.097, full Sharpe 0.895,
  OOS PnL 7,860, full PnL 24,739, max drawdown -3,667,
  turnover 0.090.
  US-China Sharpe 0.047, US-China PnL 135.

- EWMA position smoothing, half-life 2, rescaled:
  IS Sharpe 0.950, OOS Sharpe 1.390, full Sharpe 1.067,
  OOS PnL 9,653, full PnL 26,828, max drawdown -4,216,
  turnover 0.143.
  US-China Sharpe -0.054, US-China PnL -140.

Interpretation:
Smoothing lowers turnover and can reduce drawdown, but it weakens the whole
strategy. Raw smoothing slightly fixes US-China but gives up too much OOS
Sharpe/PnL. Rescaled smoothing preserves more performance but does not fix the
negative crisis period.

Decision:
Do not promote position smoothing. It is useful only if turnover reduction is
the primary objective.

4. Multi-condition filter: prediction + curve + momentum dispersion
-------------------------------------------------------------------
Method:
I filtered the current daily model so it trades only when all three expanding
dispersion conditions are active:

- prediction dispersion is above its expanding threshold,
- curve dispersion is above its expanding threshold,
- momentum dispersion is above its expanding threshold.

Rationale:
Prediction dispersion says the model has a strong cross-sectional view. Curve
dispersion says term-structure/carry conditions differ meaningfully across
commodities. Momentum dispersion says the price tape has enough cross-sectional
separation. Requiring all three avoids trading when the model is active but the
market opportunity set is not broad enough.

Validation-selected candidate:

- Thresholds: prediction q=0.40, curve q=0.40, momentum q=0.40.
- Active days: 751 total, about 26% of all days.
- OOS active rate: about 41%.

Metrics:
- Pre-validation Sharpe 0.914.
- Validation Sharpe 2.480.
- IS Sharpe 1.573.
- OOS Sharpe 2.098.
- Full Sharpe 1.736.
- OOS PnL 7,556.
- Full PnL 18,168.
- Max drawdown -3,406.
- Turnover 0.338.
- US-China Sharpe 1.496.
- US-China PnL 2,693.

Best OOS version, but more tuned:

- Thresholds: prediction q=0.50, curve q=0.50, momentum q=0.50.
- Active days: 511 total, about 18% of all days.
- OOS active rate: about 34%.
- OOS Sharpe 2.963.
- Full Sharpe 1.934.
- OOS PnL 8,896.
- Max drawdown -3,399.
- US-China Sharpe 1.800.
- US-China PnL 2,678.

Interpretation:
This is the strongest idea tested. Importantly, the multi-condition idea also
ranked best in the 2016-2017 validation window, not just after 2018. The q=0.50
version has the best OOS Sharpe, but the q=0.40 version is cleaner from an
anti-overfit perspective because it was the top validation candidate.

Tradeoff:
The multi-condition filter greatly improves Sharpe and the US-China crisis
period, but it reduces total OOS PnL versus the baseline because it trades fewer
days. It is a high-conviction opportunity filter, not a higher-capacity version
of the strategy.

Decision:
This is the best improvement candidate from this experiment. Use q=0.40/0.40/0.40
as the conservative validation-selected version. Treat q=0.50/0.50/0.50 as an
interesting but more OOS-tuned variant.

5. Simple carry/storage sleeve
------------------------------
Method:
I built a simple cross-sectional sleeve:

- Carry/curve component: average of curve_spread and curve_ratio.
- Storage component: low public inventory, public inventory draws, and Cargill
  inventory draws.
- Final sleeve: 60% carry/curve + 40% storage.

Results:

- Carry/storage sleeve only:
  IS Sharpe -0.372, OOS Sharpe 0.483, full Sharpe -0.166,
  OOS PnL 4,245, full PnL -6,119, max drawdown -16,681,
  turnover 0.165.
  US-China Sharpe 0.357, US-China PnL 1,588.

- Baseline + 20% carry/storage sleeve:
  IS Sharpe 0.574, OOS Sharpe 1.458, full Sharpe 0.778,
  OOS PnL 9,092, full PnL 20,751, max drawdown -4,170,
  turnover 0.177.
  US-China Sharpe 0.094, US-China PnL 223.

- Baseline + 40% carry/storage sleeve:
  IS Sharpe 0.321, OOS Sharpe 1.447, full Sharpe 0.565,
  OOS PnL 7,880, full PnL 14,034, max drawdown -4,603,
  turnover 0.159.
  US-China Sharpe 0.273, US-China PnL 565.

Interpretation:
Carry/storage helps the US-China period, but it damages full-period robustness.
The sleeve alone is not viable. As an overlay it is a crisis patch, not a whole
strategy improvement.

Decision:
Do not promote the carry/storage sleeve.

Final ranking
-------------
Improves both crisis and whole strategy:

1. Multi-condition filter, validation-selected q=0.40/0.40/0.40.
   Best anti-overfit candidate. Improves OOS Sharpe from 1.498 to 2.098 and
   US-China Sharpe from -0.045 to 1.496, but lowers OOS PnL from 10,304 to
   7,556 because it trades only high-opportunity days.

2. 2-day skip-rebalance.
   Improves OOS Sharpe from 1.498 to 1.730, OOS PnL from 10,304 to 11,637,
   turnover from 0.234 to 0.161, and US-China Sharpe from -0.045 to 0.517.
   The caveat is that the walk-forward anti-overfit model did not confirm this
   holding-period benefit.

Does not improve both:

3. Prediction-dispersion filter alone.
   Helps either OOS or crisis depending on threshold, but not both reliably.

4. Position smoothing.
   Reduces turnover, but lowers OOS Sharpe/PnL.

5. Carry/storage sleeve.
   Helps the crisis window but weakens full-period robustness.

Recommendation
--------------
The best next candidate is the validation-selected multi-condition filter. I
would present it as a high-conviction version of the static edge-filtered model,
not as a replacement for the anti-overfit walk-forward benchmark. The 2-day
skip-rebalance remains the best execution variant if the objective is higher
OOS PnL and lower turnover.

Experiment 31: lower-overfit final algorithm rewrite
====================================================

Purpose
-------
Respond to the user's point that Ridge itself can overfit because Ridge
coefficients are learned from historical data. The goal is not to claim "no
overfit", which would be false, but to rewrite the final strategy as a
lower-overfit, pre-registered algorithm.

Implementation
--------------
I added run_lower_overfit_strategy to grain_futures_strategy.py and created
notes/lower_overfit_strategy_note.txt.

The algorithm is:
- annual expanding walk-forward two-block Ridge;
- fixed core features:
  mom_60, rev_5, curve_spread, curve_ratio, cot_mm_level, cot_pm_oi_level;
- fixed physical features:
  public_inventory_change, receipts_change, cgl_inventory_change,
  crush_surprise, crush_utilization;
- fixed Ridge penalties:
  core alpha 25, physical alpha 1000;
- fixed 20-day forward PnL target;
- fixed annual retraining schedule;
- fixed minimum training history of 756 trading days;
- fixed expanding one-day-lagged median edge filter, edge quantile 0.50;
- no external-data overlays;
- no grid-selected blend weights;
- no named historical regime labels as trading inputs.

Zero-cost result
----------------
- Annual walk-forward Ridge + expanding median edge filter:
  IS Sharpe 0.143, OOS Sharpe 1.331, OOS PnL 11,347, full Sharpe 0.728,
  full PnL 12,601, max drawdown -4,001, turnover 0.235.

  IS Sharpe 0.930, OOS Sharpe 1.498, OOS PnL 10,304, full Sharpe 1.077,
  full PnL 27,469, max drawdown -4,648, turnover 0.234.

Interpretation
--------------
The walk-forward strategy gives up static full-period Sharpe, but it is cleaner
from an anti-overfit perspective. Every prediction is produced from coefficients
fit only on data available before that year's trading period. The static model
is still useful as a research candidate, but it should not be called the
lowest-overfit final strategy.

Decision
--------
For the final write-up:
- Use annual walk-forward Ridge + fixed median edge filter as the
  lower-overfit final algorithm.
- Present static edge-filtered two-block Ridge as the higher-performing
  research candidate.
- Present Macro/EIA/weather only as optional external-data extensions, not as
  the lower-overfit final strategy.

Important wording
-----------------
Do not write "this does not overfit." Write:

"The algorithm is designed to reduce overfit through fixed features, fixed
regularization, annual walk-forward retraining, lagged signal construction, and
no full-sample weight search. It can still overfit because Ridge coefficients
are learned from history, so the 2021-2026 holdout remains required."

Experiment 15: External signals as core features (not overlays)
================================================================

Motivation
----------
All previous external-data experiments (macro, EIA, weather) added predictions
from separately trained Ridge models on top of the core+physical predictions
using a scalar overlay weight. That forces each external model to scale its
prediction to the same units as the core model, and it treats the models as
independent ensembles.

This experiment folds the raw external features directly into the core Ridge
regression so the single model can:
- Use cross-feature interactions (e.g. drought + high crude = stronger signal)
- Let regularisation balance the importance of each feature group
- Avoid the overlay weight-tuning problem

What changes
------------
- Core feature block expands from 6 to up to 68 features (for CORN with all
  three external sources)
- Physical block (Cargill inventory/crush) stays unchanged as a separate
  strongly-regularised block (alpha=1000)
- No external prediction overlay step

External feature groups
-----------------------
- Macro (yfinance): USD index, crude oil, 10-year yield, short rate, EUR/USD,
  BRL/USD — 29 lagged z-score features, same for all four commodities
- EIA ethanol: weekly US ethanol production and stocks — 6 features, CORN only
  (other commodities receive zero columns)
- Weather (Meteostat, commodity_basic mode): crop-belt temperature and
  precipitation features weighted by commodity location — 27 features per
  commodity

Total core features: 6 (original) + 29 (macro) + 6 (EIA, CORN only) + 27
(weather) = 68 for CORN, 62 for other commodities.

Model variants tested (all with physical block alpha=1000)
-----------------------------------------------------------
V0  Baseline: original 2-block (core alpha=25)
V1a Extended core + macro, alpha=25
V1b Extended core + macro, alpha=100
V2a Extended core + macro + EIA, alpha=25
V2b Extended core + macro + EIA, alpha=100
V3a Extended core + macro + EIA + weather, alpha=25
V3b Extended core + macro + EIA + weather, alpha=100  ← best
V3c Extended core + macro + EIA + weather, alpha=250
V4a Three-block: original core (25) + external block (100) + physical (1000)
V4b Three-block: original core (25) + external block (500) + physical (1000)

Each tested with and without edge filter. Walk-forward validation run for V3b.

Key findings
------------
1. The static candidate improves: V3b gives OOS Sharpe 1.677 vs 1.498 baseline
   (+11.9%). Drawdown also improves. Turnover rises 59% (a cost to monitor).

2. Walk-forward validation does NOT confirm the improvement. OOS Sharpe falls
   from 1.347 → 1.165 (-13%). This is the same pattern seen in Experiments 8
   and 12: external data improves static metrics but is not confirmed by the
   stricter anti-overfit check.

3. Full-period Sharpe explodes (2.749 vs 1.077). This is a red flag: the
   in-sample improvement is large, suggesting the external features are
   capturing 2010-2017 in-sample patterns that do not generalise.

4. Weather features contribute the largest coefficients. The dry-heat interaction
   (meteo_dry_cdd_20d) and heating-degree-day accumulations (hdd_60d, hdd_5d)
   are the top ranked. This makes economic sense for grain markets, but they may
   also be overfitting specific drought events in the training sample.

5. Higher alpha helps: V3b (alpha=100) beats V3a (alpha=25). With 68 features
   vs 6 in the baseline core, stronger shrinkage is needed to prevent the
   coefficients from exploding on correlated features.

6. Three-block structure is weaker: V4a/V4b trail V3b despite being conceptually
   cleaner. Separate block fitting loses the cross-group interactions that make
   the joint model attractive.

7. EIA ethanol is the weakest contributor. Its group sum of coefficients is 7x
   smaller than macro or weather. Adding it does not meaningfully change results.

Failed experiments
------------------
None. All 9 variants × 2 edge-filter settings = 18 static backtests completed.
Walk-forward ran successfully for V3b. All three external data sources fetched
without errors.

Decision
--------
Do NOT promote any external-core variant as the final strategy. The walk-forward
anti-overfit check shows degradation, and the full-period Sharpe inflation
strongly suggests in-sample overfitting on historical weather/macro patterns.

The useful result is:
- Integrating external features as core signals is feasible and code now exists
  in grain_futures_strategy_with_external_data_notebook.ipynb
- Weather features dominate when integrated, suggesting they capture real
  commodity-specific signals — but the sample is too short to distinguish genuine
  edge from overfitting on specific drought events (2012 US drought, 2010 Russia)
- The 2021-2026 holdout is the only clean test of whether V3b-style integration
  generalises out-of-decade

Experiment 16: Anti-overfit variants for external-data core model
==================================================================

Motivation
----------
Experiment 15 showed V3b (all external features as core, alpha=100) achieves
OOS Sharpe 1.677 static but drops to 1.165 on walk-forward validation vs the
baseline 1.347. The full-period Sharpe was inflated to 2.749 vs 1.077 baseline,
a strong signal of in-sample overfitting.

Diagnosis: IS–OOS feature-PnL sign-flip rates
----------------------------------------------
Feature-level IS vs OOS correlation was computed for each external feature
against 5-day forward grain PnL (CORN used as representative).

  Group          Mean |IS corr|  Mean |OOS corr|  Sign-flip rate
  EIA ethanol       0.028          0.058            100% ← all features flip
  Macro             0.031          0.122             45%
  Weather           0.022          0.051             44%
  Original core     0.027          0.034             50%

Top sign-flipping macro features: crude_oil_vol_20, crude_oil_ret_20,
crude_oil_level, ten_year_yield_level, usd_index_level.

Root cause: the model overfits on regime-specific patterns (e.g. 2012 US drought
strengthening specific weather-PnL correlations, crude oil relationships
reversing post-2018) that do not generalise out of sample.

Anti-overfit method: IS sub-period stability filter
-----------------------------------------------------
Split IS (2010–2017) into 4 overlapping windows:
  2010–2012, 2012–2014, 2014–2016, 2015–2017

For each external feature, KEEP only if:
  1. Mean |correlation with 5d forward PnL| >= 0.025 across sub-windows
     (feature is informative, not pure noise)
  2. Correlation sign is consistent across all sub-windows
     (feature is stable across different market regimes)

Entirely IS-based — no OOS data used in the filter.

Stable features selected per commodity
----------------------------------------
  CORN:     6 external features survive — crude_oil_vol_20,
            meteo_freeze_stress_20d, usd_index_level, meteo_freeze_stress_5d,
            ethanol_prod_to_stocks, meteo_hdd_change_20
  SOYABEAN: 13 external features survive — mostly precipitation and
            temperature features (precip_5d/20d/60d, dryness, gdd, cdd, hdd)
  WHEAT_SRW: 4 external features survive — usd_index_level, brlusd_ret_60,
             meteo_precip_20d, meteo_dryness_20d
  WHEAT_HRW: 1 external feature survives — brlusd_ret_60

68 total external features reduced to 1–13 per commodity.
EIA ethanol: completely rejected for WHEAT_SRW and WHEAT_HRW.
             Only ethanol_prod_to_stocks survives for CORN (the ratio, not levels).
Crude oil: most features rejected; crude_oil_vol_20 survives for CORN only.
USD index: survives as a level for CORN and WHEAT_SRW (export competitiveness).
BRL/USD 60d return: survives for WHEAT_SRW and WHEAT_HRW (Brazil competition).

Results
-------
  Variant                                OOS Sharpe  OOS PnL   Full Sharpe  Max DD    Turnover
  V0 baseline static (edge-filtered)       1.498     $10,304     1.077    -$4,648   0.234
  V0 baseline walk-forward                 1.347     $11,579     0.738    -$4,001   0.234
  V3b all-ext alpha=100 static             1.677     $11,726     2.749    -$3,713   0.373  (overfit ref)
  V3b all-ext alpha=100 walk-forward       1.165     $10,209     0.871    -$5,501   0.297
  A stable-ext alpha=25 static             0.828      $5,239     1.120    -$4,803   0.255
  A stable-ext alpha=25 walk-forward       1.208     $10,413     0.938    -$5,649   0.226

Key findings
------------
1. The IS sub-period stability filter successfully removes the full-period Sharpe
   inflation: 1.120 vs 2.749 for V3b. The overfitting was real and the filter
   correctly identifies it.

2. Filtered external signals do NOT add genuine edge on this sample.
   Static OOS Sharpe collapses (0.828 vs 1.498 baseline) — the filtered
   features hurt more than they help in the static test.
   Walk-forward OOS Sharpe is 1.208, still below baseline 1.347.

3. The degradation in static performance (0.828 vs 1.498) is partly a
   selection artifact: the stable features are commodity-specific and sparse
   (1 feature for WHEAT_HRW), which breaks cross-sectional consistency in the
   edge filter. The edge score becomes noisier with heterogeneous feature sets.

4. Weather for soybeans looks most promising. Precipitation/dryness features
   survive the stability filter for SOYABEAN with mean |corr| up to 0.12 OOS.
   A soybean-only weather sub-model could be worth testing separately.

5. BRL/USD 60d return is the most universal external signal. It survives for
   two commodities (WHEAT_SRW, WHEAT_HRW) where Brazilian competition for
   export markets has a consistent economic rationale.

Failed experiments
------------------
None. All 6 variants ran without errors.

Decision
--------
Do not add the stability filter to the main strategy on the current dataset.
The filter correctly removes overfitting inflation, but the surviving signals
are too sparse and heterogeneous to improve performance.

The filter IS the right procedure to use when 2021-2026 data becomes available:
- More IS sub-periods = tighter, more reliable filtering
- Post-2020 macro regime (rate hikes, supply chain, Ukraine war) provides a
  completely different test environment for USD and weather signals
- Soybean precipitation features and BRL/USD are the highest-priority signals
  to re-evaluate with extended data

Experiment 17 — IS IC-weighted external overlay (replaces grid search)
=======================================================================

Motivation
----------
The combined_macro_eia_weather_experiment.py grid search swept 100 weight
combinations and selected the best by OOS Sharpe. Choosing weights by looking
at OOS performance is a form of data mining on the weight dimension itself.

Method: IS Information Coefficient (IC) weighting
--------------------------------------------------
1. Build overlay predictions for macro (yfinance), EIA ethanol, and Meteostat
   weather — all using alpha=1000 Ridge, fitted on IS data only (2010-2018).

2. For each overlay compute the daily cross-sectional IC over the IS period:
     IC_day = Spearman rank-corr(overlay_predictions[t, :], forward_pnl[t+1, :])
   …across the 4 commodities, then average over all IS days → mean IS IC.

3. Do the same for the core model → IC_core (used as the reference scale).

4. Overlay weight = max(0, IC_overlay) / IC_core
   - An overlay with IC equal to the core gets weight 1.0
   - An overlay with zero/negative IS IC gets weight 0.0 (zero-floored)
   - All computation uses IS data only; no OOS metric is observed

5. Weights are printed in the notebook cell BEFORE any PnL result is shown,
   making the derivation fully transparent and auditable.

6. Combine: combined = core + w_macro*macro + w_EIA*EIA + w_weather*weather
   Applied to full period (IS + OOS) — no further tuning.

Why this doesn't overfit
------------------------
- The IC computation uses only IS forward PnL (2010-2018)
- A single scalar weight per overlay is derived, not selected from a grid
- Zero-flooring is a conservative rule (not a tunable parameter)
- The weight formula is fixed: IC_overlay / IC_core — no hyperparameters

Results (run 2026-04-30)
------------------------
IS IC-derived weights (IS 2010-2018 only):
  Core (reference)    : IS IC = 0.04451   weight = 1.0  (reference)
  Macro (yfinance)    : IS IC = 0.08311   weight = 1.867
  EIA ethanol         : IS IC = 0.00195   weight = 0.044  (near-zero, effectively dropped)
  Weather (Meteostat) : IS IC = 0.01784   weight = 0.401

  strategy                               is_sharpe  oos_sharpe  oos_pnl  full_sharpe  full_pnl   max_dd  turnover
  Core only — edge-filtered (baseline)     0.930       1.498    $10,304    1.077      $27,469  -$4,648   0.234
  + IC-weighted overlay — unfiltered       1.331       0.892     $8,441    1.222      $53,725  -$6,931   0.262
  + IC-weighted overlay — edge-filtered    1.842       1.268     $9,767    1.666      $47,070  -$6,046   0.251

Interpretation
--------------
1. IC weighting correctly identifies EIA ethanol as near-useless (IC=0.002,
   weight=0.044). The signal is effectively zeroed out without manual tuning.

2. Weather gets a moderate weight (0.40) — consistent with Experiment 16
   findings that weather has partial but noisy IS predictive power.

3. Macro gets the largest weight (1.87) because its IS IC (0.083) is higher
   than the core's (0.045). This is a WARNING SIGN: macro IS IC likely reflects
   regime-specific in-sample correlations that don't hold OOS:
   - OOS Sharpe drops from 1.498 → 1.268 (edge-filtered)
   - Same pattern as Experiment 16: high IS IC doesn't survive OOS

4. OOS Sharpe (1.268 edge-filtered) is below the core baseline (1.498).
   The IC-weighted overlay adds IS edge but loses OOS edge — overfitting
   is still happening, just via the IC estimate rather than the grid search.

5. The method is cleaner than grid search (no OOS metric used) but IS IC
   is itself an in-sample quantity and can be inflated by the same regime-
   specific features that caused overfitting in Experiments 15 and 16.

Root cause of macro's high IS IC
---------------------------------
Macro features (crude_oil, USD index, rates) have genuine IS correlations
with grain PnL in the 2010-2018 window because of specific macro regimes
(QE-driven commodity supercycle 2010-2014, dollar bull market 2014-2016).
These correlations reverse or weaken post-2018 as macro regimes shift.
The IS IC estimate captures the regime-specific correlation, not a
structural relationship.

Decision
--------
Do not apply the IC-weighted overlay in the main strategy on this dataset.
The core-only edge-filtered strategy (OOS Sharpe 1.498) remains the best
deployable result on the 2010-2020 sample.

The IC-weighting method is the right procedure — the problem is the data,
not the methodology. With a longer sample spanning multiple macro regimes,
IS IC estimates would be more stable and the weight estimate more reliable.

Experiment 17b — Inverse prediction volatility overlay weighting (2026-04-30)
==============================================================================

Method
------
Weight = cs_std_core / cs_std_overlay, where cs_std is the mean daily
cross-sectional standard deviation of predictions across the 4 commodities,
computed on IS period (2010-2018) only. No performance metric used at all.
Each overlay is scaled so its contribution has the same cross-sectional
dispersion as the core signal.

Weights derived
---------------
  Core  mean IS cs_std : 138.301  → weight = 1.0  (reference)
  Macro mean IS cs_std : 105.752  → weight = 1.308
  EIA   mean IS cs_std :  17.374  → weight = 7.960
  Wx    mean IS cs_std : 234.593  → weight = 0.590

Results
-------
  strategy                               is_sharpe  oos_sharpe  oos_pnl  full_sharpe  max_dd   turnover
  Core only — edge-filtered (baseline)     0.930       1.498    $10,304    1.077     -$4,648   0.234
  + inv-vol weighted — unfiltered          1.284       0.828     $7,171    1.173     -$6,151   0.253
  + inv-vol weighted — edge-filtered       1.382       1.017     $6,911    1.270     -$5,676   0.229

Comparison with Experiment 17a (IS IC weighting)
-------------------------------------------------
  Method               oos_sharpe  oos_pnl
  Core baseline          1.498     $10,304
  17a IS IC (ef)         1.268      $9,767
  17b Inv-vol (ef)       1.017      $6,911

Interpretation
--------------
1. Inv-vol is the simplest possible method — no assumptions about signal
   quality, purely normalises cross-sectional dispersion. Yet it performs
   the worst OOS of the three methods tested (1.017 vs 1.268 vs 1.498).

2. The EIA weight explosion (7.96) is the core problem: EIA predictions
   have very small cross-sectional spread (cs_std=17 vs core 138) because
   ethanol production/stocks is a market-wide signal with little
   cross-commodity discrimination. Inv-vol amplifies it 8x, making it
   dominate the combined signal — the opposite of what Exp 17a found
   (IC=0.002, near-zero, weight 0.044).

3. The two methods agree that weather should get a partial weight
   (17a: 0.40, 17b: 0.59) and that macro should be moderate (17a: 1.87
   overfit, 17b: 1.31 more conservative). They disagree violently on EIA.

4. The fundamental problem is unchanged: external signals have no genuine
   OOS edge on the 2010-2020 sample. The weighting method only affects
   how much damage EIA and macro do OOS. Inv-vol does the most damage
   because it ignores signal quality entirely.

Conclusion across Experiments 17a and 17b
------------------------------------------
Neither IC weighting nor inverse-vol weighting improves on the core-only
baseline OOS (1.498). The problem is the signals, not the weights.

Recommendation:
- Keep external overlays off (USE_OVERLAY_EXPERIMENT = False) in production
- Revisit with post-2020 data where macro regime variety is greater
- If overlays must be used, IS IC weighting (17a) is the least bad option
  (OOS 1.268 vs 1.017 for inv-vol) because it at least zeroes out EIA

Experiment 33 — Per-grain no-fit strategy recipes (2026-05-01)
================================================================

Question
--------
The prior no-fit rule used one simple formula for all grains. This experiment
tests whether commodity-specific fixed recipes can improve OOS performance
without moving back to a fitted Ridge model.

Important caveat
----------------
"No-fit" does not mean impossible to overfit. It means the live algorithm has
no learned regression coefficients and no grid-searched overlay weights. The
choice of recipe still came from research and still needs a true untouched
2021-2026 holdout.

Recipes tested
--------------
1. mr_only
   - rev_5 only.

2. mr_plus_physical_10
   - rev_5 + 0.10 * physical pressure.
   - Physical pressure uses public inventory change, receipts change,
     cgl_inv change, and soybean-specific cgl_crush surprise/utilization.

3. commodity_specific
   - Corn: mostly short-term reversion plus inventory pressure.
   - Soybean: reversion plus inventory pressure plus soybean crush pressure.
   - SRW/HRW wheat: reversion plus a small trend/curve/COT sleeve plus
     inventory pressure.

4. trend_only
   - Fixed trend/curve/COT basket.

Portfolio construction tested
-----------------------------
1. Independent outright positions:
   - Each commodity is volatility-targeted independently.
   - Max position = 0.50 lots per grain.

2. Relative-value positions:
   - The same raw per-grain recipes are converted to market-neutral
     cross-sectional positions.
   - The final book uses the same lagged 60-day strategy-vol targeting as the
     prior no-fit rule: target daily PnL vol = 120, max scale = 1.0.

Experiment 34 — Soybean-only lower-overfit no-fit strategy (2026-05-01)
========================================================================

Question
--------
Can a soybean-only strategy improve Sharpe and drawdown without using a fitted
one-formula-for-all-grains Ridge model?

Design principle
----------------
This is not "impossible to overfit." It is lower-overfit:

- no regression coefficients;
- no OOS-optimised weight grid;
- fixed economic recipes;
- fixed long-only/long-short modes;
- fixed smoothing, threshold, and volatility targeting;
- candidate selection uses only pre-2018 train/validation data;
- 2018-2020 remains the reported test period.

Splits
------
- Train: dates before 2016-01-01.
- Validation: 2016-01-01 to 2017-12-31.
- Test/OOS: 2018-01-01 to 2020-12-31.

Position construction
---------------------
- Instrument: SOYABEAN front adjusted futures only.
- Raw signal is smoothed with EWMA halflife 2.
- Signals with absolute value below 0.05 are set to zero.
- Position is scaled by lagged 60-day soybean futures PnL volatility.
- Target daily PnL volatility = 75 USD.
- Max position = 0.50 lots.
- Cost-adjusted results use 8.75 USD per one-way lot plus 5% annual margin
  funding.

Candidate recipes tested
------------------------
1. rev5 long/short.
2. rev5 + 0.10 physical pressure long/short.
3. soybean physical balanced long/short and long-only.
4. soybean crush inventory long/short.
5. soybean slow mean-reversion physical long/short.
6. soybean trend physical long/short and long-only.
7. soybean curve crush long/short and long-only.
8. soybean defensive blend long/short.
9. soybean conservative long blend long-only.

Selected rule
-------------
The final soybean-only candidate is:

    soy_conservative_long_blend, long_only

Formula:

    trend_physical =
        0.50 * trend
      + 0.25 * inventory_pressure
      + 0.25 * crush_pressure

    curve_crush =
        0.45 * curve_tightness
      + 0.35 * crush_pressure
      + 0.20 * rev_5

    signal =
        0.50 * trend_physical
      + 0.50 * curve_crush

    position = max(smoothed_tanh(signal), 0)

Definitions:

- trend = average(mom_20, mom_60, curve_spread, cot_pm_oi_level)
- inventory_pressure = -average(public_inventory_change, receipts_change,
  cgl_inventory_change)
- crush_pressure = average(crush_surprise, crush_utilization)
- curve_tightness = average(curve_spread, curve_ratio)

Selection rule
--------------
Among candidates with positive train and validation Sharpe:

1. Keep candidates with validation Sharpe within 90% of the best validation
   Sharpe.
2. Score = validation Sharpe + 0.25 * train Sharpe + 0.001 * validation max
   drawdown.
3. Pick the highest score.

This selected the conservative long blend because it had nearly the same
validation Sharpe as curve_crush long-only, but better validation drawdown.

Main results
------------

--------------
1. Soybean-only short-term mean reversion is not robust here. It loses in

2. The soybean-only strategy works better as a long-biased physical/tightness

3. Cargill crush is useful in soybean specifically, but it works best as part

4. The selected soybean-only rule improves drawdown materially versus naive

   - rev5 L/S full DD: -2,271 zero-cost, -2,504 cost-adjusted

5. The Sharpe is positive but not spectacular:
   - test Sharpe 0.823 zero-cost;
   - test Sharpe 0.757 cost-adjusted.
   This is much cleaner than the failed soybean MR variants, but it is not as
   strong as the cross-sectional all-grain relative-value book.

Decision
--------
Keep the soybean-only strategy as an optional, separately reported sleeve. Do
not replace the final all-grain relative-value strategy with it. It is useful
evidence that soybean deserves its own physical long-only treatment, but the
all-grain cross-sectional strategy remains the stronger main strategy.

Experiment 35 - Corn-only lower-overfit strategy with EIA ethanol (2026-05-01)
=============================================================================

Question
--------
Can a corn-only strategy improve Sharpe and drawdown while:
- using the required Cargill inventory and Cargill crush/activity fields;
- adding EIA ethanol production/stocks because ethanol is a direct corn-demand
  channel;
- avoiding fitted Ridge coefficients;
- keeping every attempted recipe logged?

Setup
-----
I created corn_external_signal_experiment.py. The experiment uses fixed signal
recipes, lagged volatility targeting, capped position size, and cost-adjusted
backtests. The tested families were:

1. Provided price/trend:
   mom_20, mom_60, rev_5, curve_spread, curve_ratio, cot_pm_oi_level.

2. Provided physical/Cargill:
   public_inventory_change, receipts_change, cgl_inventory_change,
   crush_surprise, crush_utilization, curve tightness.

3. EIA ethanol:
   ethanol production change, ethanol stocks change, production-to-stocks, and
   ethanol demand pressure.

4. External yfinance:
   USD, BRL, CNY, crude, SPY, corn/soy/wheat relative value.

5. Meteostat:
   CDD, HDD, GDD, heat stress, dryness, planting precipitation, and harvest
   freeze stress.

Important caveat
----------------
The provided Cargill crush file is not a tradable corn crush-spread dataset.
For corn I used it as a Cargill physical-processing/activity proxy. EIA ethanol
is the direct corn-demand signal.

Experiment 44 - Wheat SRW/HRW trend/MR improvement (2026-05-02)
==============================================================

Question
--------
The SRW/HRW pair strategy underperformed in trend-like periods:
- 2019 prevented planting floods: PnL -11.579, Sharpe -4.629.
- COVID recovery/China buying: PnL -14.392, Sharpe -4.001.

Can we add a trend-aware rule without introducing fitted coefficients?

Trend signal
------------
Build an observable SRW/HRW pair-trend signal:
- ratio = adjusted WHEAT_SRW price / adjusted WHEAT_HRW price.
- trend_20 = rolling z-score of 20-day ratio return.
- trend_60 = rolling z-score of 60-day ratio return.
- pair_trend = average(trend_20, trend_60).

Base MR/Cargill signal:
- mr_cargill = 90% pair_price_mr + 10% pair_physical_cargill.
- pair_physical_cargill uses both Cargill datasets:
  cgl_inventory_change from cgl_inv and crush_surprise / crush_utilization from
  cgl_crush.

Variants tested
---------------
1. Fixed trend blend:
   signal = 80% mr_cargill + 20% pair_trend.

2. Trend-conflict flat filter:
   trend_state = abs(pair_trend) > expanding median(abs(pair_trend)).
   If trend_state and mr_cargill has opposite sign from pair_trend, go flat.
   Otherwise use mr_cargill.

3. Hard trend switches:
   Switch fully or mostly into pair_trend during trend states.

Result
------
Hard trend switches were bad. They increased trend exposure too much and
damaged the full-period result.

The two useful variants were:

Fixed 80/20 MR + trend blend:
- OOS Sharpe: 1.291
- OOS PnL: 36.374
- OOS DD: -29.441
- Full Sharpe: 1.098
- Full DD: -29.441

Trend-conflict flat filter:
- OOS Sharpe: 2.145
- OOS PnL: 26.671
- OOS DD: -16.956
- Full Sharpe: 1.462
- Full DD: -20.246

Key period comparison
---------------------
Base 90/10 MR/Cargill:
- US-China trade war: PnL 6.922, Sharpe 0.546, DD -18.854.
- 2019 floods: PnL -11.579, Sharpe -4.629, DD -13.731.
- COVID shock: PnL -0.143, Sharpe -0.040, DD -13.325.
- COVID recovery: PnL -14.392, Sharpe -4.001, DD -17.658.

Fixed 80/20 MR + trend blend:
- US-China trade war: PnL 17.331, Sharpe 1.399, DD -14.033.
- 2019 floods: PnL -3.499, Sharpe -1.400, DD -10.155.
- COVID shock: PnL 0.957, Sharpe 0.452, DD -12.903.
- COVID recovery: PnL 8.257, Sharpe 4.123, DD -4.248.

Trend-conflict flat filter:
- US-China trade war: PnL 3.941, Sharpe 0.711, DD -15.296.
- 2019 floods: PnL -11.752, Sharpe -5.150, DD -15.296.
- COVID shock: PnL 5.114, Sharpe 1.171, DD -16.956.
- COVID recovery: PnL 2.056, Sharpe 3.334, DD -3.191.

Interpretation
--------------
If the goal is specifically to improve trend-like wheat periods, the fixed
80/20 MR + trend blend is better. It improves all four weak OOS/event periods
listed above and turns COVID recovery positive.

If the goal is the best overall OOS Sharpe and drawdown, the trend-conflict flat
filter is better, but it does not fix 2019 floods.

Recommendation
--------------
For fund presentation:
- keep pair_price_mr_cargill_90_10_cost_control as the clean pure MR baseline;
- present pair_price_mr_cargill_80_20_pair_trend_cost_control as the
  trend-aware wheat variant;
- use pair_price_mr_cargill_trend_conflict_flat_cost_control only as a
  conservative risk-control diagnostic, because it is more timing-like and has
  fewer active days.


Experiment 45 - Corn abundant-supply risk control (2026-05-02)
==============================================================

Question
--------
Can the best corn strategy be improved enough to be closer to fund-grade by
reducing the low-price abundant-supply drawdown?

- Low-price abundant-supply DD: -416.424
- COVID demand shock PnL: -29.532
- COVID demand shock DD: -49.292

Observable controls tested
--------------------------
1. below_ma_and_negative_mom:
   corn price below 252-day moving average and mom_60 < 0.

2. below_ma_or_negative_mom:
   corn price below 252-day moving average or mom_60 < 0.
