Lower-Overfit Strategy Note
===========================

Short answer
------------
No fitted trading model can honestly be called "does not overfit." Ridge still
learns coefficients from historical data, so the correct claim is:

    This is a lower-overfit, pre-registered walk-forward algorithm.

Recommended final no-fit candidate
----------------------------------
If the priority is high Sharpe, low drawdown, and avoiding fitted Ridge
coefficients, use the no-fit Cargill-aware reversion rule:

1. Signal
   - Base signal: rev_5 short-term mean reversion.
   - Add a small fixed physical tilt:
     0.10 * average(public inventory pressure, receipts pressure,
     Cargill inventory pressure, soybean-specific Cargill crush pressure).
   - This uses both Cargill datasets:
     cgl_inv through Cargill inventory pressure;
     cgl_crush through soybean-specific crush pressure.

2. Position construction
   - Convert the fixed signal to market-neutral cross-sectional positions.
   - Risk-adjust with lagged 60-day realized commodity volatility.
   - No regression and no learned beta vector.

3. Risk control
   - Apply lagged 60-day strategy-PnL volatility targeting.
   - Target daily PnL volatility = 120.
   - Max exposure scale = 1.0.

Zero-cost result:
- IS Sharpe: 0.389
- OOS Sharpe: 1.779
- OOS PnL: 11,071
- Full Sharpe: 1.348
- Full PnL: 12,154
- Max drawdown: -2,456
- Average turnover: 0.481

Cost-adjusted result using 8.75 USD per one-way lot and 5% annual margin
funding:
- IS Sharpe: -0.177
- OOS Sharpe: 1.213
- OOS PnL: 7,502
- Full Sharpe: 0.783
- Full PnL: 7,012
- Max drawdown: -3,023
- Average turnover: 0.481

This is the cleanest answer if the reviewer is skeptical of Ridge itself. The
main weakness is turnover: the rule trades more than the Ridge strategy, so the
cost-adjusted result is materially weaker than the zero-cost result.


Recommended fitted lower-overfit benchmark
------------------------------------------
If the reviewer accepts fitted models but wants overfit control, use the annual
expanding walk-forward two-block Ridge model:

1. Data availability
   - Use only features available before the traded close.
   - Cargill inventory and crush data remain shifted by T+1.

2. Fixed feature blocks
   - Core:
     mom_60, rev_5, curve_spread, curve_ratio, cot_mm_level, cot_pm_oi_level.
   - Physical:
     public_inventory_change, receipts_change, cgl_inventory_change,
     crush_surprise, crush_utilization.
   - No macro, EIA, weather, or other external overlays in the final
     lower-overfit strategy.

3. Fixed model
   - Ridge core alpha = 25.
   - Ridge physical alpha = 1000.
   - Target = 20-day forward dollar PnL.
   - Retrain annually with an expanding window.
   - Minimum training history = 756 trading days.
   - Coefficients are fit only on data strictly before each retrain date.

4. Fixed position construction
   - Convert predictions to market-neutral cross-sectional positions.
   - Risk-adjust predictions with lagged 60-day realized volatility.
   - Demean cross-sectionally and normalize gross exposure.

5. Fixed edge filter
   - Trade only when prediction dispersion is above its expanding one-day-lagged
     median.
   - Edge quantile = 0.50.
   - This threshold is fixed, not optimized after viewing results.

Walk-forward Ridge backtest result, zero-cost
---------------------------------------------
Annual walk-forward Ridge + expanding median edge filter:
- IS Sharpe: 0.143
- OOS Sharpe: 1.331
- OOS PnL: 11,347
- Full Sharpe: 0.728
- Full PnL: 12,601
- Max drawdown: -4,001
- Average turnover: 0.235

Reference static strategy:
- OOS Sharpe: 1.498
- OOS PnL: 10,304
- Full Sharpe: 1.077
- Full PnL: 27,469
- Max drawdown: -4,648
- Average turnover: 0.234

Recommendation
--------------
Present two final candidates, depending on what the reader means by overfit:

1. No-fit final candidate:
   No-fit Cargill-aware reversion rule with volatility targeting. This has no
   learned Ridge coefficients and the best drawdown profile.

2. Fitted lower-overfit benchmark:
   Annual walk-forward two-block Ridge. This still learns coefficients, but only
   from data available before each trading year.

Present the static edge-filtered two-block Ridge as a higher-performing research
candidate, not as the most conservative final answer.

Why these are lower-overfit
---------------------------
- The no-fit rule has no learned coefficients.
- The no-fit rule uses fixed economic signs and fixed risk control.
- No full-sample coefficient fit is used for live-period predictions.
- No external-data feature expansion is used.
- No overlay weights are selected from a grid.
- No named historical regimes are used as trading inputs.
- Feature set, Ridge penalties, target horizon, retraining schedule, sizing,
  and edge filter are fixed before scoring.

Remaining risk
--------------
Even the no-fit rule can be overfit through research choices: we still chose the
rule after looking at this dataset. Ridge coefficients are also fitted from past
data. These strategies reduce overfit risk but cannot eliminate it. The honest
final sentence is:

    The algorithms are designed to reduce overfit; they still need validation
    on the untouched 2021-2026 holdout before being treated as robust.


Per-grain strategy check
-----------------------
I also tested fixed commodity-specific recipes because one shared grain formula
can be too blunt:

- Corn: short-term reversion plus inventory pressure.
- Soybean: short-term reversion plus inventory pressure plus Cargill crush.
- SRW/HRW wheat: short-term reversion plus trend/curve/COT plus inventory
  pressure.

This did not improve the result. The commodity-specific relative-value recipe
produced OOS Sharpe 1.084 versus 1.779 for the simpler no-fit Cargill-aware
reversion rule. Independent outright per-grain positions were worse and lost
money OOS.

The useful lesson is not that all grains have identical economics. The lesson is
that, in this 2010-2020 sample, the robust edge is the relative-value
cross-sectional reversion effect. Commodity-specific directional complexity adds
research degrees of freedom without adding OOS performance.

Final recommendation after the per-grain check
----------------------------------------------
Keep the no-fit Cargill-aware reversion rule as the conservative final
candidate:

    signal = rev_5 + 0.10 * physical_pressure

where physical_pressure includes public inventory, receipts, Cargill inventory,
and soybean-specific Cargill crush. Convert that signal to market-neutral
cross-sectional positions and apply lagged volatility targeting.

Use the pure mr_only relative-value result as a sensitivity check:

- Pure mr_only has slightly higher zero-cost OOS Sharpe: 1.814 vs 1.779.
- The Cargill-aware physical tilt has materially better max drawdown:
  -2,456 vs -4,213.

For the challenge write-up, I would present the physical-tilted version as the
main strategy because it uses both Cargill datasets and improves the risk
profile, while also showing the pure reversion variant to prove that the
physical tilt was not curve-fit to manufacture the whole result.


Soybean-only optional sleeve
---------------------------
I tested soybean separately because soybean is the one grain where the Cargill
crush dataset has the most direct economic meaning.

The best lower-overfit soybean-only candidate is:

    soy_conservative_long_blend, long_only

It uses no regression coefficients. It blends:

- soybean trend/curve/COT pressure;
- public inventory, receipts, and Cargill inventory pressure;
- Cargill crush surprise and utilization;
- curve tightness;
- a small rev_5 component.

The position is long-only, smoothed, thresholded, and volatility-targeted.

Results:

- Zero-cost test Sharpe: 0.823
- Zero-cost test PnL: 320
- Zero-cost full Sharpe: 0.800
- Zero-cost full max drawdown: -463
- Cost-adjusted test Sharpe: 0.757
- Cost-adjusted test PnL: 294
- Cost-adjusted full Sharpe: 0.761
- Cost-adjusted full max drawdown: -465

This improves sharply over naive soybean rev_5, which had negative test Sharpe
and much worse drawdown. However, it should remain an optional soybean-only
sleeve rather than replacing the all-grain cross-sectional strategy. The
soybean-only edge is cleaner after making it long-only and physical, but the
absolute PnL is small because the risk budget is deliberately conservative.
